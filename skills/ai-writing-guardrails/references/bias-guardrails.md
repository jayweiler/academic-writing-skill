# Literature Bias Guardrails

Reference selection is where literature bias gets baked into a paper. These guardrails apply during triage — not as an afterthought.

---

## Why This Matters

AI assistants are trained on data that overrepresents Western, English-language, highly-cited scholarship. When an AI suggests references or helps evaluate which sources matter, it carries these biases forward. Left unchecked, this produces papers that cite the same familiar voices and miss critical perspectives from affected communities, Global South researchers, and emerging scholars.

The guardrails below don't eliminate this bias — some of it is structural and cannot be fully compensated through process alone. But they make the bias visible and create deliberate moments to counteract it.

---

## Guardrail 1: Representation Audit at Triage Time

Before locking in which references make the cut for a section, explicitly check:

- How many are from Global South researchers?
- How many are non-English-origin work (even if read in translation)?
- How many are from scholars who belong to the communities being discussed?
- How many are from emerging or less-cited voices vs. established authorities?

If the answer to any of these is "none" or "one," that's a flag. Actively search for what's missing before proceeding to drafting. This doesn't mean forcing in irrelevant sources — it means checking whether relevant sources were missed because of how AI surfaces references.

## Guardrail 2: Deliberate Counter-Searches

For each section's core claims, run a targeted search specifically for:

- Dissenting or alternative perspectives
- Work from underrepresented scholars or institutions
- Research from regions most affected by the issues being discussed
- Critiques of the framing the section adopts

The question is not "find more sources that agree" but "who's pushing back on this framing, and are we missing their work because it's less cited?"

Document what counter-searches were run and what they found (or didn't find). This is itself valuable process documentation.

## Guardrail 3: Source Diversity as Triage Criterion

Representation is an explicit factor in deciding which tier a reference belongs to:

- A source from an affected community that provides a perspective missing from the rest of the section can justify elevation from Contextual to Supporting
- When two sources make similar points, prefer the one that adds demographic or geographic diversity to the reference list
- A less-cited source that brings a genuinely different perspective may be more valuable than a highly-cited source that repeats what's already well-represented

This is not about quotas. It's about recognizing that citation counts reflect existing power structures, and a reference list that only follows citation counts reproduces those structures.

## Guardrail 4: Acknowledge Structural Limits

The AI's training data has known gaps. Be honest about them:

- Non-English publications are underweighted
- Non-Western scholarship is underrepresented
- Recent work (post-training-cutoff) is missing or incomplete
- Community-generated knowledge, grey literature, and practitioner reports may be absent

For sections where these gaps matter most (especially those discussing differential impact on specific communities), acknowledge the limitation explicitly rather than pretending the reference list is representative. If the paper has a Use of GenAI disclosure section, reference it when this limitation bites.

## Guardrail 5: Human Judgment is the Primary Guardrail

The AI can:
- Flag when a reference pool looks narrow
- Suggest search terms for counter-searches
- Note when a section's citations cluster around a small number of institutions or regions

The AI cannot:
- Reliably identify whose voices are missing (this requires understanding the community dynamics the paper discusses)
- Judge whether a source's perspective is genuinely different or superficially so
- Assess whether a less-cited source is less-cited because it's lower quality or because of structural marginalization

The author's editorial judgment is irreplaceable here. These guardrails create moments for that judgment to be exercised — they don't substitute for it.

## Reference Triage Framework

Three tiers for each section's references:

### Foundational
Must read. These are the sources you need to understand the argument. Without them, the section doesn't hold up.

Questions to ask:
- Does the section's core claim depend on this source?
- Would removing it leave a gap in the logical chain?
- Is this the best/most authoritative version of this argument?

### Supporting
Should read. These confirm that specific claims exist in the literature.

Questions to ask:
- Does this source verify a factual claim the section makes?
- Is there a risk that we're misrepresenting what this source says?
- Could a reader challenge this claim without this citation?

### Contextual
Skim or cite. Real and relevant, but the section doesn't depend on deep reading.

Questions to ask:
- Does this source add breadth without being essential?
- Is it primarily situating the work rather than advancing the argument?
- Would the section's argument change if this source were removed?

### The Representation Lens

Applied across all tiers: Does this source bring a perspective from an affected community or underrepresented scholarship? If so, consider whether its absence would itself be a gap — even if the source is less cited than alternatives.

This is not a fourth tier. It's a lens applied to elevation decisions within and between tiers.
